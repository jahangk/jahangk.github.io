Portfolio Research — CS2 Item Markets

Supply elasticity, not sophistication, decides whether active management beats buy-and-hold

A walk-forward backtest of Modern Portfolio Theory across ~230 CS2 cases and sticker capsules finds that the winning strategy flips entirely depending on whether an asset's supply is fixed or ongoing — a pattern that maps directly onto momentum and collectibles literature from equity markets.

The finding

Six candidate strategies — equal weight, Sharpe-weighted, Sortino-weighted, Markowitz tangency (mean-variance optimization), a trailing-momentum tilt, and a passive cap-weighted index — were backtested on 6-month forward returns across 20 non-overlapping rebalance windows (2016–2025), on three overlapping universes: all 242 investable items, 38 cases only, and 192 sticker capsules only.

The ranking of every strategy inverts depending on the universe. The passive index dominates in capsules and the full universe. Momentum dominates in cases. Neither is close, and the reversal is total — not a matter of degree.

The explanation isn't randomness. Cases are reissued continuously; capsules are sold once, during a single Major, and never again. That single structural difference in supply elasticity is enough to flip which century-old finance heuristic applies.

Note the split in the capsules table. The index wins on raw return but has the worst Sharpe ratio of all six strategies (0.62) — nearly twice the volatility of equal weight (0.4341 vs 0.2352 std. dev.) for barely more return. Equal weight's 1.0777 Sharpe is the highest of any strategy in this entire study. The index's edge here is compensation for concentration risk, not free alpha.

Why cases and capsules diverge

Cases — elastic supply

Trend persists

Continuous reissue means price growth reflects genuine, slow-diffusing demand (case-opening culture, player base growth) rather than a supply shock. A large, unsophisticated buyer base underreacts to news, so momentum has room to continue rather than immediately revert.

Capsules — fixed supply

Trend reverts

Sold only during a single Major, never reissued. Prices spike on short-lived hype (a team's run, a streamer's coverage) in a thin, illiquid market, then revert once attention fades — a textbook attention-driven overreaction, distinct from any slower long-run scarcity trend underneath it.

Mapping to established theory

Finding
Theoretical analogue

Momentum works in cases, crashes in capsules

Jegadeesh & Titman (1993) 3–12mo momentum; Daniel & Moskowitz document momentum crashes concentrated in thin, high-volatility regimes — exactly capsules' profile.

Buy-and-hold beats active rebalancing where supply is fixed

Mirrors why cap-weighted equity indices are hard to beat: a few structural winners drive most cumulative return; periodic rebalancing gives some of it back.

Index has the highest return and highest volatility in capsules

Amihud & Mendelson — illiquidity/concentration premium. A raw-return edge earned via undiversified exposure isn't alpha, it's uncompensated risk.

Mean-variance optimization underperforms almost everywhere tested

Michaud's "Optimization Enigma" (1989) — MVO is an error maximizer; with ~20 observations against 38–242 assets, estimation noise dominates genuine signal out-of-sample.

Fixed-supply assets show boom-then-reversal

Alternative-asset literature (fine art, rare coins): passion assets show large scarcity premia and pronounced boom-bust cycles, with no supply-side dampening.

How the finding was actually reached

Three separate methodological bugs were caught and fixed over the course of this project, each of which would have quietly inverted or hidden the real result if left unaddressed.

  1. 01
    Numerically unstable covariance matrix. The original tangency-portfolio covariance used a fixed 1e-6 ridge, far too small to regularize a matrix built from fewer observations than assets. Replaced with a shrinkage estimator that guarantees a target condition number (κ ≤ 1000) via bisection, regardless of the observation-to-asset ratio.
  2. 02
    Silent, non-random sample selection. An early fix (complete.cases()) required every one of 60+ assets to report a price in the same week — satisfied in only 9 of 17–20 rebalance dates. The "winning" rolling-MVO strategy was actually averaging over a cherry-picked subsample of easy windows. Rebuilt on pairwise-complete covariance so every window is used.
  3. 03
    Parameter-sensitive conclusions. Re-running the fixed model at a looser shrinkage ceiling (κ ≤ 5000 instead of 1000) flipped the 6-month rolling-MVO ranking from last place to second. The rolling-window MVO variant was removed entirely rather than reporting a ranking that depended on an arbitrary regularization choice.

What this implies for an actual portfolio

The practical read is not "pick one strategy" — it's a two-sleeve allocation, with each sleeve run under the rule that wins in its own universe:

Cases sleeve

Momentum

Weight proportional to trailing 26-week return, positive-momentum items only, capped at 40% per item.

Capsules sleeve

Equal weight

No momentum tilt — chasing recent movers is the single worst-performing rule tested in this universe.

Open caveats before acting on this: no transaction-cost adjustment has been applied (Steam Community Market's ~13–15% cut would erode any active-strategy edge disproportionately); the capital split between sleeves is a judgment call, not backtest-derived; and every number above rests on ~20 non-overlapping six-month windows per universe — directionally consistent with theory, not statistically proven at conventional confidence levels.
